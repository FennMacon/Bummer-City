    • On Voting

    Voting is the most basic and obvious form of civic engagement, yet even people who have registered to vote fail to turn up at their polling place on election day.

    In Boston, a paltry 66.75% of registered voters cast ballots in the November 2016 General Election (which, of course, was a Presidential election); in the November 2017 General Election (an exclusively municipal election) that number dropped to 27.8% of registered voters in the city; in the May 2018 Special State Election for a State Senate seat an astounding 4.24% of Boston voters cast ballots. Believe it or not, these numbers are impressive when compared with national voter turnout averages.

    So why do so many people choose not to vote? Why do so many people fail to even register to vote? There are many culprits, but a major one is general confusion about the process. We are here to clear some things up for you.

    • Voter Registration
    • In order to vote, you need to be registered. Registration is based on your home address, as in the place where you live. It does not matter if you have an out-of-state driver's license or whatever else. If you are a student and you live in a dorm in Boston, your dorm is your mailing address and you can use that to register to vote in Massachusetts. As long as you receive mail at the address and actually live there, you're all set.
